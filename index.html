RECOGNIZING PHISHING

Learn the telltale signs of phishing emails and fake websites before attackers exploit them.

📧

Email Red Flags

  • ! Urgent language — "Act NOW or lose access"
  • ! Mismatched sender address (e.g. support@paypa1.com)
  • ! Generic greetings — "Dear Customer"
  • ! Suspicious attachments (.exe, .zip, .docm)
  • ! Requests for login credentials or SSN
  • ! Spelling and grammar errors
🌐

Fake Website Clues

  • ! URL uses HTTP instead of HTTPS
  • ! Domain typosquatting (paypa1.com, g00gle.com)
  • ! Padlock icon does NOT mean a site is safe
  • ! Poor design — pixelated logos, broken layouts
  • ! Pop-ups demanding immediate action
  • ! Missing privacy policy / contact info
⚡ INTERACTIVE: SPOT THE PHISHING EMAIL

TO: you@yourcompany.com

02

SOCIAL ENGINEERING TACTICS

Attackers exploit human psychology, not just technology. Understand their playbook.

🎭 HIGH THREAT

PRETEXTING

Attackers invent a fabricated scenario to extract information — posing as IT support, bank auditors, or HR personnel to gain your trust.

REAL WORLD CASE

In 2020, Twitter attackers called employees pretending to be IT support, convinced them to hand over credentials, then hijacked accounts of Obama, Musk, and Biden to run a Bitcoin scam netting $121,000.

🎯 CRITICAL

SPEAR PHISHING

Highly targeted attacks using personal information gathered from social media, LinkedIn, and company websites to craft convincing, personalized emails.

REAL WORLD CASE

The 2016 Podesta email hack used a spear-phishing email that mimicked a Google security alert. An aide called it "legitimate" instead of "illegitimate" — a typo that cost the DNC dearly.

📞 HIGH THREAT

VISHING (VOICE)

Phone-based phishing where attackers call victims impersonating banks, government agencies (IRS, police), or tech support to extract personal data or payment.

REAL WORLD CASE

IRS impersonation scams have cost Americans over $80 million. Attackers threaten arrest unless victims buy gift cards and read the codes over the phone.

💬 MEDIUM THREAT

SMISHING (SMS)

Phishing via text messages — fake delivery notifications, bank alerts, or prize wins with malicious links designed for mobile screens where URLs are truncated.

REAL WORLD CASE

The FedEx smishing campaign of 2021 sent millions of texts claiming a package needed a delivery address update, stealing payment info from victims who clicked the link.

💾 MEDIUM THREAT

BAITING

Physical or digital lures — infected USB drives left in parking lots, "free" movie downloads, or fake software updates that install malware when executed.

REAL WORLD CASE

A study found 45% of people plug in USB drives they find in public — a technique reportedly used to breach sensitive industrial facilities.

📷 EMERGING

QUISHING (QR CODE)

Malicious QR codes placed over legitimate ones — in parking meters, restaurants, or emailed as "multi-factor authentication" bypasses — leading to fake login pages.

REAL WORLD CASE

In 2023, the FBI issued a warning about fake QR codes at parking meters in multiple cities, redirecting payment to attacker-controlled accounts.

THE PSYCHOLOGY PLAYBOOK

⏰ URGENCY "Act in 24 hours or lose your account"
😨 FEAR "Your account has been hacked"
🤩 GREED "You've won a $1,000 gift card"
🤝 AUTHORITY "Message from your CEO / IRS / FBI"
💖 TRUST Mimics logos and branding you recognize
👥 SOCIAL PROOF "Your colleague already verified"
03

YOUR DEFENSE PLAYBOOK

Build habits that make you a hard target. These are your shields against phishing attacks.

LAYER 1
🔍

VERIFY BEFORE YOU CLICK

Hover over links to preview the real URL before clicking
Go directly to websites by typing the URL instead of clicking email links
Call the sender via a known number to confirm unexpected requests
Check the full sender email — not just the display name
LAYER 2
🔐

SECURE YOUR ACCOUNTS

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on ALL accounts
Use a password manager — unique passwords for every site
Never reuse passwords across banking, work, and social accounts
Use hardware security keys (YubiKey) for critical accounts
LAYER 3
🛡️

TECHNICAL SAFEGUARDS

Keep OS, browser, and apps updated — patches close exploits
Use email filtering (Gmail, Outlook) with anti-phishing enabled
Install reputable security software with real-time protection
Enable DNS filtering (Cloudflare 1.1.1.2, Quad9) to block malicious domains
LAYER 4
📢

REPORT & RESPOND

Report phishing emails using your mail client's built-in report button
Forward suspicious emails to reportphishing@apwg.org or phishing@irs.gov
If you clicked a link — immediately change passwords and contact IT
Report to IC3.gov (FBI) if you lost money to a scam
